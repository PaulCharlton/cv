Core Wars

Where memory is the battlefield, and code is the blade.

They taught us war in Scientific American —
not with tanks, but with REDCODE,
not with bombs, but with MOV, JMP, and SPL.
A battlefield measured in memory cells,
where every instruction was a soldier,
every cycle a heartbeat.

Core Wars.

Blue vs Red.
White vs Black.
Light vs Shadow.

I wrote programs that replicated like viruses,
forked like hydras, bombed like gods.
They spiraled through core with perfect tempo,
seeding traps, cloaking with nulls,
hunting the faint signature of a rival process
two hundred instructions to the left.

He wrote them too.
But not from a textbook.
He came from headlines —
one of the 414,
the teenage ghosts who cracked DOE labs,
nudged Sloan-Kettering’s mainframe,
and left sysadmins praying to unknown gods.

He wrote with entropy.
I wrote with balance.
He wanted annihilation.
I wanted elegance.

But both of us understood:

In Core Wars, victory isn’t surviving —
it’s outlasting in silence,
watching your opponent die one cycle at a time,
overwritten by your own shadow.

Because this wasn’t just a game.
It was a philosophy:
that every system could be mapped,
that every architecture could be subverted,
that every war begins
when one instruction reaches into the wrong space
and whispers:

“You don’t belong here.”

Core Wars wasn’t fiction.
It was the future — and it’s still running.

Dare me to do it again — I’ve already won battles you didn’t know were running.
