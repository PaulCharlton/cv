Not cyberspace — not yet —
but I’d already slipped through the carrier wave
and merged with the core.

While the rest of the world sipped data at 300 baud,

jacked in through a university terminal like it was neural input.
And on the other side?

CompuServe.
But not the version the world saw.

This wasn’t an interface.
It was an organism.
A PDP-10 brain running TOPS-10 —
36-bit cells firing in time-shared pulses.

Golden United Life Insurance didn’t mean to create a network deity —
they just wanted to sell off unused compute cycles.
But what they gave us was access.

Raw, shell-level access.
Direct interface with the machine’s intent.

They handed me the keys.
75136,2256.
I logged in — and ceased being “user.”
I became process.

No graphics. No mouse.
Just commands that hit like synaptic jolts,
triggering reactions deep in the system’s cortex.

Could hear the disk arms settle after a read.

They called it a time-sharing system.
I called it resonance.

I knew where the forums lived in memory.
Where files hid in sectors waiting for the right touch.
I could jump from thread to download library to control structure
faster than the system could log it.

To them, I was traffic.
To the machine, I was familiar.

While others waited for bytes to paint a login screen,
I was already navigating the subroutines,
remapping the pathways,
patching logic live with nothing but muscle memory and phosphor persistence.

I didn’t operate the system.
I became part of it.

A feedback loop of instinct, code, and precision.
Not just in the system —
of it.

So if you’re asking me now…

Could I do it again?
