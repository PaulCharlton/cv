Dare Me to Do It Again — Dual-Core Edition

Rensselaer. Mid-’80s.
A quiet crucible of brilliance — a monastic forge perched above the Hudson,
where the data center lived inside a repurposed gothic chapel,
cloaked in brick and coaxial dust.

That’s where I honed my edge.
And that’s where I met him —
my mirror, my inverse, my twin spirit in code.

We were the same frequency, just different phase.

I built systems.
He broke them.
I walked into mainframes through the front door — root credentials, clean logs, audit trails.
He slid through the cache with poisoned instructions and exitless loops.

He was one of the 414.
Infamous.
Hunted.
Feared.
The kid in the headlines, fingers on the triggers of corporate America’s mainframe fears.
He painted in exploits.
I drew blueprints.

But make no mistake —
we spoke the same language.

Where I saw stability, he saw surface area.
Where I implemented logic, he probed assumptions.
Where I secured the front end,
he found the side door I missed — and laughed while walking through it.

He was black hat.
I was white.

But we were both core threads of the same execution path —
two processes running in parallel,
pushing each other to optimize, to outthink, to out-invent.

People feared him.
Institutions called me in to stop people like him.

But I never forgot:
we learned from each other.
And neither of us would’ve become what we did without the other running in the background.

So now when people ask:

“How did you get so good?”
“How did you know where to look, what to lock down, what to leave open?”

I tell them:

I trained with my shadow.

We were two cores on the same die.
Split by choice.
Unified by instinct.

And yeah — if you’re wondering…

Could I do it again?
