On Trust: The $30 Million Follow-Up

How one saved email unlocked a hidden acquisition -- and why I refused to sign until they made it right.

-- Part III: The Call --

Late July - 13 months after full departure

Thirteen months of silence. No contact. No updates. Nothing.

Then a stranger called, offering $800,000 for "all of your shares."

For shares in a company that had pushed me out, changed its name, and gone dark? That number didn't add up. Something was very wrong -- or very right.

I called my attorney.

When someone offers you money unprompted, the real number is always bigger.

-- Part IV: The Discovery --

We exercised shareholder rights -- demanded full financial disclosure, governance documents, cap tables, the works.

They declined. They stalled. For weeks.

Then a different law firm called -- not representing the startup, but representing the acquirer. A public company had offered to buy the entire startup in a stock swap valued at $90 million.

The acquiring company's lawyers were doing diligence on the cap table and discovered my 20% stake -- which management had never disclosed to them.

The $800,000 "offer" was an attempt to buy me out at a fraction of fair value before the deal closed.

That's not negotiation. That's fraud by omission.

Your cap table is a legal document. If someone hides your ownership from an acquirer, it can kill the deal.

-- Part V: Hardball, Round Two --

The acquirer needed my written consent to close. I had real leverage this time.

My attorney reminded them of two outstanding obligations:
1. An unpaid final invoice.
2. A share certificate that was never delivered.

They pushed back. Pointed out I had $12 million in upside. Why make waves?

Their logic was sound. But my principle was better.

I dug up that email -- the one where my co-founder had written, in his own words, "termination for convenience." My attorney attached it to a cover letter citing excused performance and legal entitlement to the remaining shares.

They caved. The certificate arrived the next day.

I signed. The acquisition closed weeks later -- at $150 million in public stock.

"Termination for convenience" is a legal term. When someone puts it in writing, they're handing you a receipt for everything they still owe you.

-- Epilogue --

A share certificate worth north of $30 million sat on my nightstand for a while. A bit surreal.

The acquiring company later peaked at 10x that valuation. Then cratered to 1% of peak. Timing is everything.

Three things I took from this:

1. Protect your equity with physical instruments, not promises. If you can hold a certificate, hold a certificate.

2. Save every email. The paragraph that saved me was written by the person who betrayed me. People document their own misconduct more often than you'd think.

3. When trust breaks, restructure the relationship -- don't just walk away empty-handed. Rage feels good. Leverage feels better.
